What I Wish I Knew Before Taking My First Shot

Starting photography feels exciting—like you’re finally stepping into a new way of seeing the world. But it can also feel overwhelming. Aperture? ISO? Which camera to buy? Should you be editing everything? Should you even be posting yet?

Take a breath. Whether you're holding a phone, a used DSLR, or a brand-new mirrorless camera, you're in the right place. Here's what I wish someone told me when I picked up my first camera—before the gear obsessions, before the blur, before the YouTube rabbit holes.

Gear Doesn’t Matter (Yet)

I know. The first thing you did (and I did too) was Google “best camera for beginners.” But here's the truth: Any camera is a good starting point. Your smartphone, your grandparent’s old film camera, or a used entry-level DSLR from a pawn shop—all of them can teach you how to compose, how to observe light, and how to tell a story.

Instead of chasing the next lens or body, focus on learning what your current gear can do. Understanding exposure, focus, and framing will take you much further than upgrading your kit every few months.

Try this: Challenge yourself to shoot with only one camera and one lens (or just your phone) for a week. Learn what its limits are—and how to push them.

Light is Everything

No, seriously. Light is everything. It’s what turns a dull snapshot into a photo that feels alive. Before you even think about settings, start observing how light behaves. Notice how shadows shift during the day. Watch how the color of light changes from sunrise to sunset.

When you start to “see” light, your photos instantly get better—no matter your gear or skill level.

Try this: Shoot the same subject three times in one day—morning, midday, and golden hour—and notice the difference.

Auto Mode Is a Tool, Not a Trap

There’s no shame in using Auto mode. It's there to help you learn. Think of it like training wheels—not something to rely on forever, but a way to stay balanced while you build confidence.

Use Auto, but pay attention to what your camera is doing. Look at the settings it chooses in different situations. Then start experimenting with Aperture Priority (A/Av) or Shutter Priority (S/Tv) to take more control, one step at a time.

Try this: Take one photo in Auto, then try to recreate it in Manual or Aperture Priority. Compare the results—and the process.

Don’t Fear the Messy Shots

Your early photos might be blurry, overexposed, too dark, or just plain boring. That’s normal. You’re learning a visual language, and you need to take “bad” photos to find your voice.

So don’t delete everything that doesn’t look perfect. Keep those mistakes. Look at them. Learn from them. You’ll be amazed at how quickly you grow when you stop trying to be good, and start trying to be curious.

Try this: Set a goal to take 100 photos this week—and don’t delete any of them. Review them later, and write down what you liked and didn’t like.

Share Early, Share Often

You don’t need to be an expert to share your work. In fact, sharing your early work is part of becoming an expert. Feedback, encouragement, and even simple likes can help you stay motivated.

More importantly, sharing helps you see your progress over time. Don’t wait until your photos are “good enough.” Start now. Let people see you grow.

Try this: Start a small Instagram account or online album just for your photography journey. Give yourself permission to post without pressure.

Your first roll—whether it’s actual film or a fresh SD card—isn’t about perfection. It’s about exploration. You’re learning how to see in a new way. That takes time, and every blurry photo is part of that process.

So keep shooting. Keep learning. Keep clicking that shutter. Your best photos are still ahead of you.
